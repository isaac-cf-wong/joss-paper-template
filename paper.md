# Summary

Replace this paragraph with a plain-language summary of what your software does
and who it is for. Avoid jargon here; JOSS reviewers include readers from
outside your specialty [@joss:paper].

# Statement of need

Describe the research problems your software addresses, who will use it, and how
it fits into existing workflows [@example:repo].

# State of the field

Compare your software to other commonly used tools in the area. If similar
packages exist, explain why you created a new project (or fork) rather than
extending an existing one, and what scholarly contribution is unique to your
work [@example:article].

# Software design

Explain meaningful design trade-offs: architecture, extensibility, performance,
dependencies, and how those choices support your research application. This
should go beyond a superficial tour of modules or file layout [@joss:paper].

# Research impact statement

Provide specific evidence of impact (citations, adoption, integrations,
benchmarks, teaching use) or a credible, concrete case for near-term
significance. Avoid vague claims without support [@example:article].

# AI usage disclosure

State explicitly whether generative AI was used in software development,
documentation, or manuscript preparation. If it was used, describe where and how
you verified correctness [@joss:paper].

# Acknowledgements

Acknowledge funding, contributors, and any institutions or projects that
supported this work.
